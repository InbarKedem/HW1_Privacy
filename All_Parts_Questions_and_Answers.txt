--- QUESTION 1.5 (Report) ---

You have just removed all direct identifiers — `df_no_pii` cannot be tied to a 
named individual by a single column. Yet privacy harm is still possible.

Describe **two distinct concrete privacy harms** that could result from 
publishing `df_no_pii` (i.e., the dataset *after* direct identifiers have been 
removed, but with no further protection). The two harms must be **different in 
kind** — not two examples of the same underlying mechanism.

For each harm explain:

(a) **Who is harmed** — which individuals or groups in the dataset?

(b) **What additional information** the attacker needs (if any) beyond the 
released dataset, and how plausible it is that they have it.

(c) **What the actual harm consists of** — be specific (e.g., re-identification, 
attribute disclosure, group-level inference, discrimination, etc.).

Generic statements like "privacy is violated" do not count.

ANSWER FOR Q1.5:

HARM 1: Re-Identification via Linkage Attack (Attribute Disclosure)
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

(a) **Who is harmed**: 
Individuals in the dataset whose demographic combination (specifically Zip + Age 
+ Sex) is sufficiently rare or distinctive to be uniquely matched with external 
public records. This is particularly severe for individuals with uncommon 
demographic combinations. Research by Latanya Sweeney (1997) showed that about 
87% of the US population can be re-identified using Zip + Date of Birth + Sex 
alone.

(b) **What additional information the attacker needs**:
The attacker needs access to external public or semi-public datasets containing 
demographic information such as:
  - Voter registration rolls (publicly available in most US states)
  - Census bureau microdata or aggregated census records
  - Public social media profiles linking names to demographics
  - Telephone directories or address databases
  - Professional directories (LinkedIn, academic directories)
  
These sources are widely accessible and plausible for attackers to obtain. 
Voter registration databases can often be purchased cheaply or accessed for a 
small fee. Social media data can be scraped. This information availability is 
well-documented in privacy research.

(c) **What the actual harm consists of**:
This is **re-identification followed by attribute disclosure**. Once an 
attacker matches a record in df_no_pii to an external record using the Zip+Age+Sex 
quasi-identifiers, they can identify the real name and personal details of the 
individual. Then, they can infer sensitive attributes from the dataset such as:
  - The individual's exact income level (from Target column)
  - Educational background
  - Occupation
  - Financial information (capital gains/losses)
  - Work hours

The harm manifests as:
  - **Privacy violation**: The individual's sensitive information is now linked 
    to their real identity without consent.
  - **Targeted discrimination**: An employer, insurer, or lender could use this 
    information to discriminate against the individual.
  - **Harassment or stalking**: Bad actors could use re-identified information 
    for targeted harassment.
  - **Financial harm**: Information could be sold to third parties or used for 
    predatory targeting.

Example: An attacker could identify that a specific named person (matched via 
Zip+Age+Sex) has high capital gains and low education, enabling wage 
discrimination or targeted scams.

HARM 2: Group-Level Attribute Inference & Demographic Targeting/Discrimination
────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

(a) **Who is harmed**:
Groups of individuals defined by combinations of quasi-identifiers 
(e.g., all females aged 25-35 in zip code 02118, or all individuals in a 
specific workclass-education combination). The harm affects the group collectively 
and individuals within it through discrimination based on group statistics.

(b) **What additional information the attacker needs**:
**No additional external information is required.** The attacker only needs the 
released df_no_pii dataset itself. They can directly query, filter, and analyze 
the data to extract group-level statistics without needing to re-identify 
individuals.

(c) **What the actual harm consists of**:
This is **group-level attribute inference and demographic targeting**, distinct 
from individual re-identification. Even without knowing individual identities, 
an attacker can:

1. **Extract group statistics**: Filter rows by quasi-identifiers (e.g., "Sex==F 
   AND Age in [25,35] AND Zip==02118") and compute aggregate statistics such as:
     - Average income in that demographic group
     - Distribution of occupations
     - Education levels most common in that group
     - Employment patterns and work hours

2. **Enable discrimination**: Employers, lenders, insurers, or marketers can use 
   these group-level inferences to:
     - Deny opportunities to entire demographic groups based on group statistics
     - Charge higher premiums to risky groups
     - Target predatory lending or marketing to groups with high debt
     - Implement discriminatory hiring practices

3. **Unfair statistical inference**: Even though individual identities are not 
   revealed, statements like "85% of unmarried women aged 30-40 in this zip code 
   earn <$50K" can lead to unfair assumptions and discrimination.

4. **Intersectional harm**: Groups defined by intersecting quasi-identifiers 
   (e.g., "non-white females in tech roles") can face amplified discrimination 
   based on revealed group patterns.

Example: A credit card company could analyze df_no_pii to find that individuals 
in a specific zip code + race + age combination have high capital losses. They 
could then refuse to grant credit to anyone matching those demographic criteria, 
even if that individual has good credit history—a discriminatory practice enabled 
by the group-level inference.

--- QUESTION 2.4c (Report) ---

Compare the three numbers `n_unique`, `n_unique_no_zip`, `n_unique_no_age`. 
Which of the three QIs (`Zip`, `Age`, `Sex`) carries the **most identifying 
power** in this dataset, and why? Briefly relate your answer to a practical 
decision: *if the research team has only enough "utility budget" to generalize 
one QI aggressively, which one should they choose?*

ANSWER FOR Q2.4c:

**Data Summary:**

The three identifiability metrics from the linkage attack analysis are:

- `n_unique` (full QI: {Zip, Age, Sex}):    32,457 uniquely identifiable (99.68%)
- `n_unique_no_zip` ({Age, Sex} only):      5 uniquely identifiable (0.02%)
- `n_unique_no_age` ({Zip, Sex} only):      27,086 uniquely identifiable (83.13%)

**Which QI carries the most identifying power: ZIP CODE**

**Why Zip is most identifying:**

1. **Magnitude of impact**: Removing Zip code from the quasi-identifier set 
   reduces re-identification risk from 99.68% to just 0.02%—a drop of 99.66 
   percentage points. This is an enormous collapse in identifying power.

2. **Contrast with other QIs**: By comparison:
   - Removing Age only reduces risk from 99.68% to 83.13% (a 16.55 percentage 
     point drop)
   - Sex contributes similarly to Age via its role in the QI combination

3. **Why Zip dominates**: Zip codes are geographically precise identifiers with 
   sparse distributions. Each zip code covers a limited geographic area with a 
   specific set of residents. When combined with Sex (a binary attribute), Zip 
   creates fine-grained equivalence classes. In contrast, Age is a relatively 
   coarse attribute with many people sharing the same age, and Sex is binary 
   (only two values globally).

4. **Quasi-identifier interaction**: While the full QI set {Zip, Age, Sex} 
   achieves 99.68% identifiability, the dominance of Zip is evident: {Zip, Sex} 
   alone already achieves 83.13% identifiability, whereas {Age, Sex} achieves 
   only 0.02%.

**Practical Recommendation for Privacy Budget Allocation:**

If the research team has only enough "utility budget" to aggressively generalize 
ONE quasi-identifier, they should prioritize **ZIP CODE**. 

The reasoning:
- Zip generalization (e.g., truncation to 3-digit prefix, or grouping into 
  broader regions) directly addresses the dominant source of identifying power.
- This single generalization would reduce re-identification risk from 99.68% to 
  ~0.02%, achieving near-complete privacy protection with minimal additional 
  utility loss from generalizing Age and Sex.
- Conversely, if they generalized only Age, the risk would remain at 83% due to 
  {Zip, Sex} combinations. If they generalized only Sex, the risk would also 
  remain very high.
- Zip generalization is thus the highest-impact, most cost-effective privacy 
  intervention they can make.
